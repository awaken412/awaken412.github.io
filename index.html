References

1. The dying Gaul, by Epigonus (?) 3rd C.BC

This marble statue is one of the most popular to come down to us from antiquity, not for its value as a historical document or for its particularly features, but because of how it captures and evokes emotion and empathy. The vast majority of people have an understandably complex relation with death, and one facet of that is our imagining of how we wish to die, not only in terms of circumstance but also, and perhaps more importantly, in our deportment leading up to the final instant. Moreover, it is something that equalizes us by turning us into things, and we cannot go back from that.

2. Marc Seguin artworks

Marc Seguin is a French Canadian artist and writer. He likes to explore darker themes relating to destruction, terrorism, dictatorship and death in his work. One series of paintings centers around depictions of people being attacked by taxidermied roadkill, bringing to mind the uncovering of things hidden (instinct, bodies) and on the other side of the natural world from us. Another relevant series are his paintings of bombed-out ruins, painted using the ashes of his friend’s mother. To paraphrase him, it’s a way of using death to show a trace of the life it has snuffed out.

3. My Body, Your Room by Sandro Masai & Sune Petersen

My Body, Your room is an audio-visual interactive installation and dance performance in which the body of the dancer influences the sound and light in the room. The heart rate of the performer controls the blinking of the lights and the volume of the sound. This project plays with the idea of giving «life » to an inanimate object (the room) by reading biometric data. Without the performer, the interactive art installation doesn’t exit. We would also like to use the heart rate of a person to activate our artwork, and their heart rate would be the central point of our project.
